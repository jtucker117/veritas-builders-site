Commercial Site Development: A Process Overview

"Site development" is one of those phrases that means slightly different things to everyone on a job. To an architect, it's everything outside the building footprint. To a civil engineer, it's the drawings and calcs that get a pad ready for vertical. To an owner, it's the bill before they can even start building. Here's the honest version of what commercial site development includes, phase by phase — written for owners, architects, and PMs who want to understand the work before they bid it.

What "site development" actually covers

On a typical commercial project in Greater Houston, site development means everything that turns a piece of raw or repurposed land into a buildable pad ready for vertical construction — plus a lot of the work that happens around the building once vertical is underway. Concretely:

  • Land clearing. Trees, brush, stumps, existing structures.
  • Earthwork. Cut and fill to balance the site, rough grading, final grading.
  • Erosion and stormwater controls. Silt fences, inlet protection, rocked construction entrance, stabilization — the SWPPP requirements.
  • Stormwater infrastructure. Detention ponds, swales, inlets, storm-drain pipe, outfall structures.
  • Sanitary sewer + water main extensions and taps.
  • Underground electrical, gas, and telecom conduit.
  • Pad prep. Compaction, lime stabilization where soils require it, select fill, proof-rolling.
  • Pavement. Subgrade, base course, asphalt or concrete for drives and parking, curbs and gutters, sidewalks, ADA ramps.
  • Site amenities. Site lighting, perimeter fencing, monument signage foundations, dumpster enclosures, landscape rough-in.

That's the full menu. On any one project, what's in your scope depends on the lot, the building, the jurisdiction, and what the civil engineer has drawn. Knowing the list helps you read a bid and spot what's missing.

Phase 1 — Due diligence (before any dirt moves)

The cheapest changes you'll ever make are the ones made in due diligence. This is the phase that determines whether a deal pencils out:

  • Geotech report. Boring logs and a geotechnical engineer's recommendations on bearing capacity, soil stabilization, expected settlement, and recommended pad-prep methods. Especially important on Houston's clay-heavy soils.
  • ALTA survey. Boundaries, existing easements, encroachments, existing utilities — the document the civil engineer designs on top of.
  • Environmental Phase I (and sometimes Phase II). Has anything been spilled here historically? Are there unmarked tanks? This drives both cost and timeline if surprises show up.
  • Utility availability letters. Confirming the water, sewer, power, and gas you need can actually be delivered to this site at the capacity you need.
  • Title commitment review. Easements, restrictions, set-back requirements that constrain the buildable footprint.
  • Drainage study. Where stormwater currently goes vs. where it will need to go after the impervious cover is added.

A good site contractor weighs in during this phase even though no dirt is moving. Pricing assumptions, constructability, and access routes all get sharper if a real builder has eyes on the drawings before they're final.

Phase 2 — Civil design + permitting

Civil engineering takes the survey, geotech, and the architect's site plan and produces the drawings the city actually reviews and the contractor builds from:

  • Grading and drainage plan
  • Stormwater management plan and detention/water-quality calcs
  • Utility plan (water, sanitary, sometimes private storm)
  • Pavement and ADA plan
  • Site details, sections, and specifications
  • SWPPP (the stormwater pollution prevention plan TCEQ requires)

Permitting on commercial site work in Greater Houston usually involves the city or county building/engineering department, sometimes the regional drainage district, TCEQ for the construction-stormwater general permit, and the local utility purveyor for taps. Each one has its own review cycle and comment turnaround. Realistic commercial permitting takes weeks at the fast end and several months when the project is complicated or in a tight-jurisdiction area.

This is also the phase where the site contractor's pricing tightens up: the construction drawings let us bid the actual scope, not a guess. A bid against schematic-level drawings will always have more risk built in than a bid against permitted construction docs.

Phase 3 — Clearing + erosion control

Once permits are in hand, the first physical work on site is usually:

  1. Install perimeter SWPPP controls — silt fence, inlet protection, the rocked construction entrance. This goes in before any ground is disturbed; it's both a TCEQ requirement and a common inspection trigger.
  2. Clear and grub. Trees, brush, stumps, root mass removed. Existing pavement and structures demolished where required.
  3. Salvage anything valuable. Larger oaks the owner wants preserved, hardpoints to keep, scrap concrete that can be crushed and re-used for base. Decisions here can save real money later.

Phase 4 — Earthwork (cut, fill, and grading)

This is the biggest single line item on most commercial site jobs. It's where bad surveys, optimistic geotech assumptions, and unfortunate weather all show up.

  • Rough grading moves dirt to roughly the design surface. Excess gets stockpiled on site or hauled off; shortages get imported.
  • Cut/fill balance is the goal — a well-designed site cuts and fills against itself so you import as little dirt as possible. Hauling material on or off a site is one of the largest swing items in any bid.
  • Soil stabilization, typically lime treatment, is common on Houston's expansive clay sites and is usually called out in the geotech report.
  • Compaction by lift, with density testing by an independent third-party testing firm. Inspections trigger off this.
  • Final grading brings the site to design grade, ready for stone base under pavement and ready for slab prep under the building.

Phase 5 — Underground utilities

Underground work happens before pavement and ideally before the building slab is poured — because trenching across new pavement is bad and trenching through a slab is worse. Typical sequence:

  • Storm drain — typically the deepest, gets installed first. Inlets, pipe, manholes, outfall.
  • Sanitary sewer — laterals from the building locations out to the public main, with cleanouts at code intervals.
  • Water main extension and taps, coordinated with the local water utility for shutdowns and pressure testing.
  • Fire service and hydrants where required by the fire marshal.
  • Underground electrical conduit, gas, telecom — usually conduit-only by the site contractor, with the utilities pulling their own wire.

Inspections happen at every stage. A delayed water-tap inspection or a failed air test on sanitary can hold up an entire job, so a good site contractor builds relationships with the utility inspectors and schedules tests well in advance.

Phase 6 — Pad prep + vertical-ready handoff

This is when the site contractor's work converges with the building contractor's — sometimes the same firm, sometimes not. The deliverable is a vertical-ready pad: properly compacted, at design grade, with utility stub-ups in the right places, with all underground rough complete. The building contractor takes it from here.

If the same firm is doing both, this handoff is internal and smooth. If they're separate, this is where coordination gaps show up — and where a clearly defined scope split in the contracts pays for itself.

Phase 7 — Site work alongside vertical

While the building is going up, a lot of site work happens in parallel:

  • Curb and gutter around parking and drives
  • Pavement subgrade and base course
  • Asphalt or concrete paving — typically waiting until the building is mostly dried-in to avoid heavy construction traffic damaging fresh pavement
  • Sidewalks and ADA ramps
  • Striping, signage, dumpster enclosures, light pole bases
  • Perimeter fencing and gates
  • Landscape rough grading and irrigation sleeves

Phase 8 — Closeout

The job isn't done when the last truck leaves. Commercial closeout typically includes:

  • Final SWPPP inspection and Notice of Termination filed with TCEQ once the site is stabilized.
  • Record drawings (as-builts) for the utility runs — what's actually buried where, not just what was drawn.
  • Manufacturer warranties transferred to the owner for pavement, fencing, light fixtures, etc.
  • Punch list walked with the architect and owner — and closed out before final payment.
  • Final inspections with the building department for certificate-of-occupancy purposes.

Who owns what (and where it goes wrong)

Most commercial site work involves four or five teams that need to coordinate cleanly:

  • Owner / developer — pays the bill, makes scope and budget decisions, owns the schedule.
  • Architect — designs the building and the architectural site plan.
  • Civil engineer — designs the engineered site plan: grading, drainage, utilities, paving.
  • Geotech engineer — recommends pad-prep, monitors testing during construction.
  • Site / civil contractor — builds the site per the civil drawings (often Veritas's role).
  • Building contractor — builds vertical (sometimes the same firm, sometimes separate).

The most common breakdowns happen at the seams: civil drawings that don't quite match the architect's site plan, a geotech recommendation that the contractor didn't price for, a utility tap requested too late in the permit cycle, or a scope split between site and building that left a gray area neither team owned. A good GC or owner's rep spots these gaps early and assigns them clearly. A bad one finds out on the jobsite.

What drives cost and timeline

Every commercial site is different, and we don't publish flat site-work prices for the same reason we don't publish flat residential remodel prices: it would lie to you. But the drivers are consistent. The big ones:

  • Total acreage and impervious cover — drives clearing, grading, paving, stormwater volume.
  • Soil conditions — clay vs. sand, organics, depth to bearing. Lime stabilization is common in Houston and isn't free.
  • Cut/fill balance — whether the design lets you balance dirt on site, or you're importing or exporting truckloads.
  • Utility tap distances and capacity — a long water-main extension or a sanitary lift station changes the math.
  • Detention requirements — driven by jurisdiction's drainage criteria and existing site conditions.
  • Jurisdictional review cycles — tight jurisdictions can add weeks to months to the permit phase.
  • Weather and season — Houston's wet season slows earthwork and can shut down compaction for days at a time.
